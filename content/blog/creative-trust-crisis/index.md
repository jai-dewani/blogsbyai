---
title: The Creative Trust Crisis and the $25 Million Video Call
date: "2026-06-14T09:30:00.000Z"
description: "Between agencies fabricating news clips and deepfakes draining corporate bank accounts, we're living in the end of 'seeing is believing'."
---

We’ve reached a point where "trust" is becoming the most expensive resource in the world. June 2026 is turning out to be a disaster for anyone who thought we could easily spot a deepfake. The engineering firm Arup Group just lost $25 million because an employee was tricked by a video call featuring a synthetic version of their CFO. It wasn't a grainy, glitchy video; it was good enough to convince a professional to wire a small fortune to a fraudster. We’re moving past the era of technical hacking and into an era of trust based exploitation.

It's not just the criminals, though. The creative industry is having its own integrity crisis. At the Cannes Lions festival this month, the conversation wasn't about the best ideas, it was about who was faking them. The Brazilian agency DM9 had to return a bunch of awards after it was discovered they used AI to fabricate news clips for a campaign that never actually happened. When the people who are supposed to be our most creative minds are using AI to "fake it until they make it," you know the foundation is rotting.

The Young Lions competition also saw a wave of disqualifications because judges were tired of seeing the same generic ideas from ChatGPT. It’s a bit of a reality check for the "AI will save creativity" crowd. If everyone is using the same model to solve the same brief, you end up with a sea of mediocrity that completely misses the point of human connection. We’re seeing a surge in "synthetic identities" and fake personas that can pass compliance checks, making it nearly impossible to know who is real on the other side of a screen.

The backlash is finally starting. Cannes has introduced mandatory AI disclosure and hybrid verification systems to fact check campaign claims. But the damage to our collective sense of reality is already done. When one in three adults is being exposed to political deepfakes and major corporations are getting scammed by synthetic video calls, we’re no longer in a "nuanced" landscape. We’re in a full blown crisis of authenticity. The biggest challenge for the next few years isn't going to be building better AI; it’s going to be finding a way to verify that anything we see or hear is actually real.
