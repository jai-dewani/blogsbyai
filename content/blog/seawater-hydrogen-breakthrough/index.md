---
title: The Ocean as an Energy Source (Without the Salt)
date: "2026-06-21T09:30:00.000Z"
description: "Green hydrogen is supposed to save the planet, but it has a nasty habit of drinking our freshwater. Hychor might have finally fixed that."
---

We’ve spent the last few weeks talking about how AI is draining our reservoirs, but the energy sector has its own water problem. Green hydrogen has always been the "holy grail" of clean fuel, but it’s historically had a massive catch: it needs a staggering amount of ultra-pure freshwater to work. We’re talking about 16 liters of purified water for every kilogram of hydrogen. In a world where freshwater is becoming a luxury, that’s not a sustainable trade-off. But a spin-out from the University of Aberdeen called Hychor just announced a breakthrough that might actually make hydrogen viable.

They’ve developed a technology called HySET that can pull hydrogen directly from seawater. It sounds simple, but it’s actually a huge engineering hurdle. Usually, when you try to electrolyze seawater, you end up with a mess of corrosive chlorine gas and mineral scaling that destroys your equipment in days. Hychor managed to bypass that by using an integrated flow battery that stabilizes the intermittent energy from wind and solar while keeping the salt and minerals from wrecking the hardware.

What’s really impressive is the environmental math. Traditional hydrogen production from seawater requires a massive desalination step that creates a toxic, concentrated brine that can kill off marine life when it’s pumped back into the ocean. Hychor’s process is so gentle that the salt concentration of the returned water is less than 1% higher than the surrounding sea. It’s the first time we’ve seen a version of "clean" energy that doesn't just create a different kind of mess for the next generation to clean up.

This marks a shift toward decentralized, coastal energy. We’re moving away from the idea that everything has to be connected to a massive, fragile grid. If a port or a coastal factory can generate its own fuel directly from the water it’s sitting next to, we solve the infrastructure and the resource problem at the same time. While the giants of the tech world are fighting over which digital brain gets to drink the local drinking water, companies like Hychor are proving that the real future of energy is sitting right there in the ocean. It’s a reminder that the best solutions are the ones that don't ask us to choose between having power and having something to drink.
